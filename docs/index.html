Species-agnostic and Salmonella-specific Models for Antimicrobial Resistance Prediction Using FCGR and ResNet-18

Saifelden M. Ismail, Salma H. Fayed

Abstract

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) prediction from bacterial genomes remains a major challenge for clinical microbiology and surveillance. We developed deep learning models based on Frequency Chaos Game Representation (FCGR) and a ResNet-18 architecture to classify resistance phenotypes directly from whole-genome assemblies. Using homology-aware clustering to prevent genomic data leakage, we compared a species-specific model for Salmonella enterica with a species-agnostic model spanning S. enterica, Escherichia coli, and Staphylococcus aureus. The Salmonella enterica model achieved high predictive accuracy, particularly for cephalosporins, while performance was lower for tetracycline and ampicillin. The species-agnostic model generalized less effectively overall but improved prediction for underrepresented species such as E. coli. Benchmarking against the gene-based tool ResFinder confirmed that FCGR-based models can approach curated predictions while capturing distributed genomic features not restricted to known resistance genes. This suggests that deep learning can highlight genomic patterns with potential biological relevance beyond established markers. Overall, species-focused models give the most reliable performance, while species-agnostic approaches can boost prediction for poorly sampled pathogens and aid AMR surveillance.

Materials and Methods

Comprehensive pipeline from genomic data to resistance predictions

1. Data Collection

Whole genome sequences were curated from NCBI and public repositories with validated phenotypic resistance profiles. The final dataset included 10,079 genomes across three species (S. aureus, S. enterica, E. coli).

2. FCGR Encoding

Genomes were transformed into 256×256 grayscale images using 8-mer frequency analysis. This alignment-free encoding captures both local sequence motifs and global compositional biases.

3. Model Training

A modified ResNet-18 architecture was trained with homology-aware splitting and cross-validation. Two models were developed: (i) a Salmonella-specific model across 8 antibiotics, and (ii) a species-agnostic model across 3 species for Gentamicin and Tetracycline.

4. Validation

Evaluation metrics included AUC, MCC, and balanced accuracy to account for incomplete resistance profiles. Confusion matrices and per-class metrics were generated for both models.

Results and Discussion

Performance evaluation across species and antibiotics

Salmonella enterica-Specific Model

The Salmonella-only model achieved strong overall performance with balanced accuracy of 82% and AUC of 89%. Performance varied significantly across antibiotics: near-perfect predictions for cephalosporins (cefoxitin, ceftiofur, ceftriaxone) with balanced accuracy ≥94%, while tetracycline and ampicillin showed more modest performance (71-79% balanced accuracy).

Species-Agnostic Model

The general model trained on 10,079 genomes across 3 species achieved balanced accuracy of 73% overall. Despite sparse data for E. coli (85.98% missing tetracycline labels), the model maintained reasonable predictive performance. Notably, it consistently outperformed species-specific models for E. coli, suggesting successful cross-species learning from Salmonella genomic patterns.

Clinical and Biological Implications

Our deep learning approach demonstrates significant clinical potential for rapid AMR screening. Benchmarking against ResFinder revealed that FCGR-based models can approach curated gene-database predictions while potentially capturing distributed genomic features beyond known resistance genes. Saliency analysis showed that while the model recognizes established resistance genes like blaCMY-2, it relies on additional genomic motifs, suggesting potential for novel resistance mechanism discovery.

  • Reduction in diagnostic time from days to hours without requiring resistance gene annotations
  • Species-agnostic models can improve predictions for underrepresented pathogens
  • Potential for discovering novel resistance mechanisms through distributed genomic patterns
  • Scalable deployment across diverse clinical and surveillance settings
