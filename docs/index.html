Deep Learning-Based Prediction of Antimicrobial Resistance from Whole Genome Sequences

Saifelden Mohamed, Salma Hatem

Zewail City of Science and Technology, Giza, Egypt

Abstract

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) poses a critical threat to global public health, necessitating rapid and accurate diagnostic methods. We present a comprehensive deep learning framework for predicting antimicrobial resistance directly from bacterial whole genome sequences. Our approach employs Frequency Chaos Game Representation (FCGR) to transform variable-length genomic sequences into fixed-size numerical representations suitable for convolutional neural network analysis. Using a modified ResNet18 architecture, we evaluated our model across six clinically relevant bacterial species: Staphylococcus aureus, Salmonella enterica, Escherichia coli, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, and Acinetobacter baumannii. The framework achieved accuracies ranging from 72% to 95% across 66 antibiotics, with particularly strong performance for S. enterica (>95% accuracy). Our species-agnostic model demonstrated 91% accuracy, suggesting potential for generalized AMR detection across diverse bacterial pathogens. This work contributes to the growing body of evidence supporting machine learning approaches for rapid AMR screening in clinical settings.

Materials and Methods

Comprehensive pipeline from genomic data to resistance predictions

2. FCGR Encoding

Frequency Chaos Game Representation (FCGR)

FCGR provides an alignment-free method for encoding genomic sequences as numerical matrices. Each genome is represented as a 256×256 matrix where each element corresponds to the normalized frequency of a specific 8-mer. This representation captures both local sequence patterns and global compositional biases while maintaining computational efficiency.

Results and Discussion

Performance evaluation across species and antibiotics

Species-Specific Models

Individual models trained for each species demonstrated robust performance with accuracies ranging from 72% (M. tuberculosis) to 95% (S. enterica). The variation in performance correlates with dataset size, genomic complexity, and the quality of phenotypic annotations.

  • Gram-positive bacteria: 85-90% mean accuracy
  • Gram-negative bacteria: 82-95% mean accuracy
  • Mycobacteria: 72% mean accuracy

Species-Agnostic Model

The unified model trained on all species achieved 91% overall accuracy, demonstrating the potential for developing generalized AMR prediction systems. Key findings include:

  • Consistent performance across beta-lactam antibiotics
  • Reduced accuracy for aminoglycosides
  • Strong generalization to unseen species combinations

Clinical Implications

Our deep learning approach demonstrates significant clinical potential for rapid AMR screening. The model's high sensitivity ensures minimal false negatives—critical for preventing treatment failures—while maintaining specificity to reduce unnecessary broad-spectrum antibiotic use. Key clinical advantages include:

  • Reduction in diagnostic time from days/weeks to hours
  • Independence from existing resistance gene databases
  • Potential for novel resistance mechanism discovery
  • Scalable deployment across diverse clinical settings
